**Political Science Comprehensive Study Guide**
This study guide provides a synthesized overview of fundamental political concepts, ideologies, and institutional theories. It serves as a rigorous academic resource for understanding the structures of power, governance, and the theoretical frameworks that define modern political science.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**I. Politics, Power, and Authority**
The study of political science begins with the distinction between the nature of political activity and the mechanisms of influence.
**1. Conceptual Definitions and Scholars**
- **David Easton:** Defined politics as the **"authoritative allocation of values and interests"** (權威性的價值與利益分配) within a society. His "Political Systems Theory" views politics as a macro-process of value distribution.
- **Andrew Heywood:** Represents the modern textbook approach, focusing on politics as an activity involving the distribution of power, the role of the state, and the functioning of institutions. Heywood’s work emphasizes that politics is the study of how power is organized and used for specific purposes.
- **Hannah Arendt:** Her work, such as *On Violence*, distinguishes between power and violence. In the broader tradition of political theory, Arendt’s perspective focuses on the communicative and collective nature of political action within the public sphere.
- **Power vs. Authority:**
  - **Power:** The ability to influence or control the behavior of individuals or groups.
  - **Authority:** The recognized and "legitimate" right to exercise power, often derived from institutional roles or legal frameworks.
**Contemporary Example:** The **democratization of Taiwan** (lifting of Martial Law) represents a shift in authority from a centralized authoritarian structure to a multi-party democratic system where the "authoritative allocation of values" is determined through public participation and elections.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**II. The Five Major Political Ideologies**
Ideologies are sets of ideas and principles that explain how society should work and provide a blueprint for social order.
**Core Tenets and Oppositions**
| Ideology | Core Tenets | Primary Opposition |
|----|----|----|
| **Liberalism** | Individualism, civil liberties, property rights, and the rule of law. Includes Classical, Social, and Neo-liberalism. | Opposes **Socialism/Marxism** regarding private property and state intervention. |
| **Conservatism** | Tradition, social order, stability, and skepticism of radical change. Focuses on social and national preservation. | Opposes **Liberalism** and **Marxism** over the pace of change and the rejection of established traditions. |
| **Socialism** | Equality, collective interest, social welfare, and state-led redistribution of wealth. | Opposes **Liberalism** on the grounds that unrestrained markets create inequality. |
| **Marxism** | Historical materialism, class struggle (Bourgeoisie vs. Proletariat), and the eventual overthrow of capitalism to reach a classless society. | Opposes **Capitalism** and **Conservatism**; views Liberalism as a "superstructure" for ruling class interests. |
| **Nationalism** | National identity as the primary unit of political loyalty, national sovereignty, and the right to self-determination. | Can oppose **Globalism** or **Liberal Internationalism** when they threaten national sovereignty. |
**Contemporary Example: Brexit (The United Kingdom)** serves as a modern conflict between Nationalism (sovereignty and border control) and Liberal Internationalism (regional economic integration and the free movement of people).
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**III. Theory of the State: Sovereignty and Structure**
**1. Sovereignty**
**Jean Bodin** defined sovereignty as the essential attribute of a state—supreme, indivisible, and absolute. In modern political science, sovereignty is the state's ultimate legal authority over its territory and population.
**2. State vs. Government**
- **The State:** A permanent entity characterized by a defined territory, a permanent population, and a sovereign government. It is an abstract structure of power.
- **The Government:** The temporary administrative body or leadership that exercises the power of the state. Governments change through elections or transitions, while the state remains.
**3. State Forms**
- **Unitary State:** Power is concentrated in the central government (e.g., the United Kingdom, Taiwan).
- **Federal State:** Power is constitutionally divided between a central government and regional/state units (e.g., USA, Germany).
- **Confederal State:** A loose alliance of independent states that delegate limited power to a central authority (e.g., historical confederations).
**Contemporary Example:** The **United States of America** operates as a **Federal State**, where individual states like California or Texas hold significant power over local education and law enforcement, distinct from the federal government in Washington D.C.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**IV. Models of Democracy: Majority vs. Consensus**
Democracy can be categorized by how decisions are reached and how power is shared.
**1. The Majority Model (Majoritarianism)**
- **Mechanism:** Focuses on "winner-takes-all." The majority (or plurality) of voters wins the right to govern.
- **Characteristic:** Centralized power, often associated with two-party systems and the Westminster model.
**2. The Consensus Model**
- **Mechanism:** Emphasizes power-sharing, coalition building, and the protection of minority interests.
- **Characteristic:** Proportional representation, multi-party systems, and a focus on broad agreement rather than simple majority rule.
**Contemporary Example: Belgium or Switzerland** utilizes a **Consensus Model** to manage diverse linguistic and cultural groups through shared governance, whereas the **United States** often utilizes a **Majority Model** in its legislative and executive elections.
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
**V. New Institutionalism**
New Institutionalism rejects the idea that political outcomes are merely the sum of individual choices, emphasizing that **institutions matter** and shape behavior.
| School of Thought | Key Argument |
|----|----|
| **Rational Choice Institutionalism** | Individuals are utility-maximizers who follow rules because institutions provide incentives and constraints that make certain choices more beneficial. |
| **Historical Institutionalism** | Focuses on "Path Dependency." Decisions made at critical historical junctures create institutions that persist over time and determine future political trajectories. |
| **Sociological Institutionalism** | Institutions are shaped by culture and norms. Political actors follow rules not just for benefit, but to maintain "legitimacy" and social appropriateness. |
**Contemporary Example:** The **US Congress** can be analyzed through **Historical Institutionalism** to understand how its centuries-old procedural rules (like the filibuster) continue to shape modern legislative gridlock through path dependency.

**Glossary of Important Terms**
- **Alienation (疏離):** A Marxist concept describing the separation of individuals from their humanity and the products of their labor under capitalism.
- **Base/Superstructure (下層建築/上層建築):** The Marxist model where the economic base determines the legal, political, and religious superstructure of society.
- **Behavioralism (行為主義):** A movement in political science focusing on observable political actions rather than legal or institutional descriptions.
- **Civic Society (公民社會):** The collection of non-governmental organizations and institutions that manifest the interests and will of citizens.
- **Democratization (民主化):** The transition from an authoritarian regime to a democratic one.
- **Legitimacy (正當性):** The quality of being accepted as right, proper, or valid; the basis upon which authority is exercised.
- **Multi-party System (多黨制):** A political system where multiple parties have the capacity to gain control of government offices, often requiring coalitions.
- **Sovereignty (主權):** The supreme and absolute power by which any independent state is governed.

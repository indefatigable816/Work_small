Based on the provided rulebook and virology sources, here is a structured list of viruses you should know, along with their key "trigger words" and clinical associations used in competitive medical microbiology.
**I. DNA Viruses**
These viruses generally replicate in the nucleus (except Poxviruses) and many can establish latent infections.
- **Adenoviridae (Adenovirus)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Pharyngitis, conjunctivitis (pink eye), military recruits, icosadeltahedral capsid with fibers, atypical pneumonia.
- **Papillomaviridae (Human Papillomavirus - HPV)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Warts, koilocytes, cervical cancer, STD, CIN (Cervical Intraepithelial Neoplasia), E6/E7 proteins.
- **Polyomaviridae**
  - **JC Virus:** PML (Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy), demyelination, opportunistic in AIDS.
  - **BK Virus:** Kidney, hemorrhagic cystitis in transplant patients.
  - **Merkel Cell Virus:** Merkel cell carcinoma.
- **Parvoviridae (Parvovirus B19)**
  - **Trigger Words:** B19, fifth disease, "slapped cheeks" rash, aplastic crisis (in sickle cell patients), hydrops fetalis.
- **Poxviridae**
  - **Smallpox (Variola):** Synchronous vesicles, bioterror agent, Guarneri bodies.
  - **Molluscum Contagiosum:** Umbilicated papules, only infects humans.
  - **Trigger Words:** Large complex structure, cytoplasmic replication, zoonosis.
- **Hepadnaviridae (Hepatitis B Virus - HBV)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Dane particle, HBsAg, HBeAg (indicator of replication), reverse transcriptase, chronic, hepatocellular carcinoma.
- **Herpesviridae**
  - **HSV-1 & HSV-2:** Neurotropic, Cowdry type A inclusion bodies, syncytia, vesicles, Tzanck smear.
  - **VZV (Varicella-Zoster):** Chickenpox (all stages of lesions), Shingles (single dermatome).
  - **EBV (Epstein-Barr):** B-cell lymphotropic, heterophile-positive mononucleosis (Monospot test), atypical lymphocytes (Downey cells), Burkitt lymphoma.
  - **CMV (Cytomegalovirus):** "Owl’s eye" inclusion body, heterophile-negative mononucleosis, congenital infection.
  - **HHV-6 & HHV-7:** Roseola infantum, exanthem subitum, rapid onset of high fever.
  - **HHV-8 (KSHV):** Kaposi sarcoma, AIDS-associated.

**II. RNA Viruses**
RNA viruses mostly replicate in the cytoplasm (except Orthomyxoviruses and Retroviruses).
**Positive-Sense Single-Stranded RNA (+ssRNA)**
- **Picornaviridae**
  - **Poliovirus:** Anterior horn motor neurons, flaccid paralysis.
  - **Coxsackie A Virus:** Herpangina, hand-foot-and-mouth disease.
  - **Coxsackie B Virus:** Pleurodynia (Bornholm disease), myocarditis.
  - **Rhinovirus:** Common cold, acid-labile (unlike other picornaviruses), replicates at 33°C.
  - **Hepatitis A Virus (HAV):** Infectious hepatitis, fecal-oral, abrupt onset.
- **Caliciviridae (Norovirus/Norwalk)**
  - **Trigger Words:** "Winter vomiting disease," cruise ships, institutional outbreaks, watery diarrhea.
- **Coronaviridae**
  - **Trigger Words:** Solar corona-like, common cold, SARS (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome), MERS.
- **Togaviridae**
  - **Rubella Virus:** German measles, cataracts/deafness (Congenital Rubella Syndrome), vaccine (MMR).
  - **Alphaviruses:** Mosquito-borne, encephalitis (WEE, EEE, VEE).
- **Flaviviridae**
  - **Dengue:** "Breakbone fever," shock syndrome (DSS), antibody-dependent enhancement.
  - **Yellow Fever:** Jaundice, black vomit, Councilman bodies.
  - **West Nile Virus:** Bird reservoir, encephalitis in elderly.
  - **Zika Virus:** Microcephaly, GBS (Guillain-Barré Syndrome), sexual/congenital transmission.
  - **Hepatitis C Virus (HCV):** Posttransfusion hepatitis, chronic (70%), hepatocellular carcinoma.
**Negative-Sense Single-Stranded RNA (-ssRNA)**
- **Orthomyxoviridae (Influenza)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Segmented genome (8 segments), antigenic shift (pandemics), antigenic drift (epidemics), Hemagglutinin/Neuraminidase.
- **Paramyxoviridae**
  - **Measles (Rubeola):** CCC & P (Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis, Photophobia), Koplik spots, SSPE.
  - **Mumps:** Parotitis, orchitis, aseptic meningitis.
  - **RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus):** Infant bronchiolitis/pneumonia, syncytia.
  - **Trigger Words:** F protein (fusion), syncytia formation.
- **Rhabdoviridae (Rabies Virus)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Bullet-shaped, Negri bodies, hydrophobia, animal bite (zoonosis).
- **Filoviridae (Ebola & Marburg)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Filamentous virion, severe hemorrhagic fever, BSL-4.
- **Bunyaviridae (Hantavirus)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Rodent excreta (inhalation), pulmonary syndrome.
- **Arenaviridae (Lassa & LCMV)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Sandy appearance (ribosomes in virion), rodent reservoir.
**Double-Stranded RNA (dsRNA)**
- **Reoviridae (Rotavirus)**
  - **Trigger Words:** Segmented (11 segments), double-double (dsRNA + double capsid), infantile diarrhea, "wheel-and-spoke" morphology.

**III. Retroviruses**
- **Retroviridae**
  - **HIV-1/2:** AIDS, CD4 T cells \< 200/μL, opportunistic infections (e.g., *Pneumocystis*), RT (Reverse Transcriptase), integration into host DNA.
  - **HTLV-1/2:** Adult T-cell leukemia (flower cells), tropical spastic paraparesis.
**IV. Prions**
- **Trigger Words:** Infectious protein, spongiform encephalopathy (CJD, Kuru), no nucleic acid.

Antiviral drugs are categorized based on which stage of the viral replication cycle they inhibit, and their names often contain specific **suffixes or word parts** that indicate their pharmacological class.
**1. Protease Inhibitors (PIs)**
These drugs block the viral protease enzyme, which is essential for cleaving polyproteins into functional units for mature virion assembly.
- **Naming Clue:** Names typically end in **"-navir"** (derived from "no virus").
- **Examples:** Saquinavir, ritonavir, indinavir, and darunavir (used for HIV). For **Hepatitis C (HCV)**, names often end in **"-previr"** such as simeprevir, boceprevir, and telaprevir.
**2. Integrase Strand Transfer Inhibitors (INSTIs)**
These medications prevent the viral integrase enzyme from inserting the viral genome into the host cell's DNA.
- **Naming Clue:** Names typically contain **"-tegravir"**.
- **Examples:** Raltegravir, dolutegravir, and bictegravir.
**3. Neuraminidase Inhibitors**
Used primarily for influenza A and B, these drugs block the neuraminidase enzyme, preventing the release (budding) of new virus particles from the host cell.
- **Naming Clue:** Names typically end in **"-mivir"**.
- **Examples:** Oseltamivir (Tamiflu), zanamivir, and peramivir.
**4. Nucleoside and Nucleotide Analogs**
These are structural mimics of the building blocks of DNA/RNA. They work by competing for the active site of viral polymerases and acting as **chain terminators** to stop genome replication.
- **Naming Clues:** Many anti-herpetic drugs end in **"-cyclovir"** or **"-ciclovir"**. Anti-HIV versions often have abbreviations like **"-vudine"** or **"-ine"**.
- **Examples:** Acyclovir, valacyclovir, and ganciclovir (Herpes/CMV). Zidovudine (AZT) and lamivudine (3TC) (HIV).
**5. Entry and Fusion Inhibitors**
These drugs prevent the virus from attaching to or entering the host cell by blocking receptors or fusion proteins.
- **Naming Clue:** CCR5 coreceptor antagonists often end in **"-viroc"**.
- **Examples: Maraviroc** (entry inhibitor) and **Enfuvirtide** (T20, a fusion inhibitor).
**6. Biologic Agents (Monoclonal Antibodies)**
Some antiviral therapies utilize lab-created antibodies to neutralize extracellular virions.
- **Naming Clue:** Names end in **"-mab"**, with specific prefixes indicating the source:
  - **-omab:** Fully mouse/murine sequences.
  - **-ximab:** Chimeric (part mouse, part human).
  - **-zumab:** Humanized (mostly human).
  - **-umab:** Fully human sequences.
- **Example:** Palivizumab (used for Respiratory Syncytial Virus/RSV).

T cell development
Sunday, March 29, 2026
1:15 AM

T-cell development is a highly orchestrated process that begins with the migration of progenitors from the bone marrow to the thymus, where they undergo a rigorous series of selection events to ensure they are both functional and self-tolerant before being exported to the periphery.
**I. Progenitor Stage: Bone Marrow to Thymus Entry**
- **Location:** Hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) originate in the **bone marrow**.
- **What Happens:** A multipotent lymphoid progenitor (the early thymic progenitor or ETP) exits the bone marrow and travels via the blood to the **thymus**. These cells enter the thymus parenchyma near the **corticomedullary junction (CMJ)**.
- **Key Molecular Event:** Upon entry, progenitors encounter **Notch ligands** on the thymic epithelium. Signaling through the Notch receptor is essential for committing these precursors to the T-cell lineage and repressing alternative fates like B-cell development.
**II. Double-Negative (DN) Stages: Lineage Commitment (Cortex)**
Thymocytes at this stage are called "double-negative" because they lack both **CD4 and CD8** markers. They migrate from the CMJ toward the outer cortex.
- **DN1 (ETP):** The cell is still multipotent and migrates from the CMJ to the cortex.
- **DN2 (Pro-T):** The cell becomes irreversibly committed to the T-cell lineage. Rearrangement of the **TCR \$\beta\$, \$\gamma\$, and \$\delta\$ gene loci** begins.
- **DN3 (Pre-T):** A critical checkpoint. Thymocytes either become **\$\gamma\delta\$ T cells** or continue toward the \$\alpha\beta\$ lineage. Cells that successfully rearrange a \$\beta\$-chain pair it with a surrogate **pre-T\$\alpha\$** chain to form the **pre-TCR**.
- **DN4:** Assembly of the pre-TCR triggers rapid proliferation in the subcapsular cortex, halts further \$\beta\$-chain rearrangement (**allelic exclusion**), and initiates maturation to the **double-positive (DP)** stage.
**III. Double-Positive (DP) Stage: Selection (Cortex)**
- **Location:** DP thymocytes reside deep within the **thymic cortex**.
- **What Happens:** These cells now express both **CD4 and CD8** and begin rearranging the **TCR \$\alpha\$-chain**. Once a complete \$\alpha\beta\$ receptor is expressed, the cell undergoes two major selection processes:
  - **Positive Selection:** DP thymocytes "browse" MHC molecules on **cortical thymic epithelial cells (cTECs)**. Only those that bind self-MHC/peptide with low-to-intermediate affinity survive, ensuring **MHC restriction**.
  - **Negative Selection:** Cells that react too strongly with self-antigens are induced to undergo apoptosis (**clonal deletion**) to prevent autoimmunity, ensuring **self-tolerance**.
  - **Death by Neglect:** Roughly 90–95% of DP thymocytes fail to recognize any MHC/peptide complex and die.
- **Lineage Commitment:** During positive selection, the cell "chooses" its final functional fate. Recognition of **MHC Class I** signals the cell to become a **CD8+ cytotoxic T cell** (using the Runx3 factor), while **MHC Class II** recognition leads to a **CD4+ helper T cell** (using the ThPOK factor).
**IV. Single-Positive (SP) Stage: Final Maturation (Medulla)**
- **Location:** Mature single-positive cells move into the **thymic medulla**.
- **What Happens:** Medullary thymic epithelial cells (mTECs) use the **AIRE transcription factor** to express "tissue-restricted antigens" (like insulin). This allows the thymus to perform a final round of negative selection against antigens that only exist in distant organs. Some self-reactive cells are diverted at this stage to become **regulatory T cells (Tregs)**.
- **Emigration:** Mature T cells exit the thymus via blood vessels at the CMJ. This is regulated by the **S1PR1 receptor**.
**V. Peripheral Stage: Recirculation and Activation**
- **Location:** Secondary lymphoid organs (spleen, lymph nodes, MALT).
- **Status:** Cells are mature but **naive**, meaning they have not yet encountered their specific foreign antigen.
- **Activation:** When a naive T cell encounters an activated **dendritic cell** presenting its cognate antigen, it requires three signals for full activation: (1) TCR engagement, (2) costimulation (e.g., CD28), and (3) polarizing cytokines.
- **Expansion & Effector Fate:** Following activation, T cells undergo **clonal expansion** (rapid division) and differentiate into **effector cells** (to fight current infection) or **memory cells** (for long-term protection).

Resistance to phagocytosis
Wednesday, March 11, 2026
3:45 PM

The ability to evade **phagocytosis** is a critical virulence factor that allows pathogens to survive, multiply, and disseminate within the host. Pathogens have evolved diverse strategies to interfere with various stages of the phagocytic process, from initial chemotaxis to final intracellular digestion.
The following table summarizes pathogens that escape phagocytosis based on their specific mechanisms:
**Pathogen Evasion of Phagocytosis**
| **Mechanism** | **Pathogens** | **Specific Factors and Details** |
|----|----|----|
| **Inhibition of Chemotaxis** | *Streptococcus pyogenes*, *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* | Production of enzymes like **C5a peptidase** that cleave chemoattractants; toxins like **streptolysin** inhibit leukocyte movement. |
| **Prevention of Ingestion (Antiphagocytic Surface)** | *Streptococcus pneumoniae*, *Haemophilus influenzae*, *Neisseria meningitidis*, *Cryptococcus neoformans*, *Bacillus anthracis* | **Polysaccharide capsules** mask surface patterns (PAMPs), repel phagocytes, and inhibit complement activation. *S. aureus* **Protein A** binds the Fc region of IgG to prevent opsonization. |
| **Inhibition of Phagosome-Lysosome Fusion** | *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, *Legionella pneumophila*, *Chlamydia spp.*, *Toxoplasma gondii*, *Brucella spp.* | Pathogens release components or use specialized secretion systems (e.g., **Type III**) to block the fusion of the phagosome with bactericidal lysosomes. |
| **Escape from Phagosome into Cytoplasm** | *Listeria monocytogenes*, *Shigella flexneri*, *Rickettsia spp.*, *Francisella spp.*, *Trypanosoma cruzi* | Production of pore-forming toxins or hemolysins (e.g., **listeriolysin O**) to rupture the phagosomal membrane and enter the nutrient-rich cytosol. |
| **Survival within Phagolysosome** | *Mycobacterium leprae*, *Salmonella typhimurium*, *Coxiella burnetii*, *Leishmania spp.* | Possession of **waxy, lipid-rich cell walls**; production of **catalase and SOD** to detoxify reactive oxygen species; or adaptation to the acidic pH. |
| **Killing the Phagocyte (Cytotoxicity)** | *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Streptococcus pyogenes*, *Bacillus anthracis* | Release of potent toxins such as **leukocidins**, **streptolysins**, or **α-toxin** that induce lysosomal discharge into the cytoplasm or cause direct cell lysis. |
| **Biofilm Formation** | *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*, *Staphylococcus epidermidis*, *Candida albicans* | Secretion of **extracellular polymeric substances (EPS)** creates a physical barrier that prevents phagocyte penetration and hides microbial antigens. |
| **Antigenic Masking and Variation** | *Trypanosoma brucei*, *Neisseria gonorrhoeae*, *Schistosoma spp.*, *Borrelia spp.* | Constant shifting of surface proteins (e.g., **VSG** in trypanosomes); or acquiring a coating of **host molecules** (MHC, blood group antigens) to appear as "self". |
**Clinical Significance**
- **Virulence:** Pathogens requiring a low infective dose, such as *Shigella* (only 100 organisms), are considered highly virulent because they efficiently overcome these innate defenses.
- **Chronic Infection:** Mechanisms like inhibition of phagosome-lysosome fusion allow pathogens like *M. tuberculosis* to establish lifelong, latent infections within the host's own immune cells.
- **Pus Formation:** Pyogenic bacteria (e.g., *Staphylococci*) that kill large numbers of neutrophils lead to the accumulation of dead cells and debris, resulting in **pus**.

**Hill’s criteria of causation (1965)**
\(1\) Strength of association § The magnitude of the association and its statistical strength
\(2\) Consistency § The persistent finding of an association between exposure and outcome in multiple studies of adequate power, and in different persons, places, circumstances, and times.
\(3\) Specificity § single (or few) effect(s) of one cause, or no more than one possible cause for one effect
\(4\) Temporality § The occurrence of a cause before its purported effect. § Temporality is the sine qua non for causal explanation.
\(5\) Biological gradient (dose response) § An increment in effect with an increase in the strength of the possible cause provides strong support in favor of a causal hypothesis
\(6\) Plausibility, Coherence, and Analogy § A proposed causal relationship not violate known scientific principles, and that it be consistent with experimentally demonstrated biologic mechanisms and other relevant data. § Biological understanding
\(7\) Experimental evidence § An experiment is typically a situation in which a scientist controls who is exposed in a way that does not depend on any of the subject’s characteristics. § Natural experiment

Cohort study: advantages
1\. Temporality
2\. Obtain incident rate/risk
3\. Less bias due to prospective evaluation of exposures;
4\. Evaluate multiple outcomes;
5\. Efficient for rare, uncommon exposures
Cohort study: disadvantages
1\. a longer observation time, especially for chronic diseases ➢ Low incidence and long latency periods
2\. Response rate & loss to follow-up (need to motivate/encourage cohort participants staying in contact for repeated assessment)
3\. Cost/manpower: funding issue
4\. Difficult to identify non-exposed cohort
5\. Relatively complex analysis (e.g., competing risk)

# rct is randomized but why researchers still want data to be balanced on controlled variables
Researchers aim for balance on controlled variables in Randomised Controlled Trials (RCTs) primarily to increase statistical precision, reduce variance, and ensure that accidental imbalances do not obscure the true treatment effect. While randomization balances known and unknown covariates on average over many trials, it may not perfectly balance them in a single, small study.
Key Reasons for Balancing Controlled Variables:
Increased Precision and Power: Including covariates—especially baseline measures of the outcome variable—reduces unexplained variance. This allows researchers to detect smaller treatment effects with a smaller sample size (i.e., higher statistical power).
Correction of Small Sample Imbalance: In small-scale trials, randomization can still result in uneven distribution of prognostic factors between groups (e.g., one group having significantly older patients).
Improved Accuracy of Results: Even if randomization is valid, researchers often use analysis techniques like stratification, block randomization, or covariate adjustment in regression to "clean up" any chance imbalances, leading to a more reliable estimate of the treatment effect.
Increased Confidence: Ensuring groups are balanced on known predictors of the outcome (e.g., age, disease severity) provides greater confidence that the observed results are entirely due to the intervention.
Commonly Controlled Variables:
Baseline Outcome Measures: The most important covariate to include.
Prognostic Factors: Variables like age, gender, or disease severity that influence the outcome, regardless of the treatment.
Site/Location: To control for variation in care across different centers.
Note: While balancing is good, researchers should not over-control by including variables affected by the treatment itself, which can introduce bias.
